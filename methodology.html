Methodology
How We Calculate Everything
A complete walkthrough of the scoring system, decision tree, data sources, and assumptions behind every number in the De-Extinction Explorer. Every score is computed automatically from raw species data, i.e. no values are hardcoded.
1 Genomic Similarity
Whole-genome DNA similarity Source data

The DNA similarity % is the primary genomic input. It represents the percentage of the genome that is identical between the extinct species and the candidate living relative, determined via whole-genome alignment using NCBI BLAST multiple sequence alignment.

A higher similarity means fewer total gene edits are required to reconstruct the extinct phenotype from the candidate's genome. Because genomes are large and edit efficiency is imperfect, even a 0.5% difference can translate to thousands of distinct loci requiring modification.

⚠ Similarity alone is not sufficient to determine viability. A 99% similar candidate in a completely incompatible habitat will fail. All five ecological factors are equally important. See Section 2.
Divergence time Source data

Divergence time (in millions of years, Mya) is derived from published phylogenetic studies and calibrated molecular clocks. It measures how long ago the extinct and candidate lineages last shared a common ancestor.

More recently diverged species share more identical regulatory regions, gene expression patterns, and developmental pathways, making genome reconstruction substantially easier. The divergence score uses a logarithmic decay so that differences at short timescales (0.1 vs 1 Mya) matter more than differences at long timescales (25 vs 30 Mya).

The divergence time used in this website is gathered from a dataset and not calculated ourselves.
2 Ecological Composite Score
How the composite score is calculated Ecological

The Ecological Composite Score (0–100) is a weighted average of seven independent factors. Each factor is scored independently based on raw species data from the candidate and the extinct species. Weights reflect the relative importance of each factor to the survival of a reintroduced population.

Eco score = Σ (factor score × factor weight) across all 7 factors
Factor Weight How it is scored Why this weight
Climate match 20% Degree of overlap between extinct and candidate temperature ranges (°C). Full overlap = 100, no overlap = 0. Climate mismatch is the most immediate physiological barrier. A candidate that cannot thermoregulate in the target habitat cannot survive.
Habitat intactness 20% % of the extinct species' historic habitat that remains ecologically intact today (0–100). Sourced from IUCN habitat assessments and GBIF SDM. Even a perfect biome match fails if the habitat is severely degraded. Fragmentation, deforestation, and permafrost loss can make restoration impractical.
Biome match 15% Exact biome = 100. Adjacent/related biome = 55. Unrelated biome = 15. Biome determines the full suite of vegetation, prey, and microclimate conditions. A candidate from a mismatched biome faces cascading adaptation challenges.
Food availability 15% % availability of the extinct species' historic prey or food sources in the target habitat today (0–100). Population viability depends directly on food web support. Without adequate prey or forage, even a well-adapted species will decline rapidly.
Human pressure 10% Land-use pressure in the target range (0–100 scale, higher = worse). Score is inverted: low pressure = high score. Sourced from GBIF occurrence data. Long-term survival requires stable habitat free from intensive agriculture, hunting, or urban expansion. Human conflict is often the primary cause of reintroduction failure.
Diet compatibility 10% Exact diet type match = 100. Broadly compatible diet = 60. Incompatible = 10. A candidate with an incompatible diet cannot fill the extinct species' ecological niche, reducing the ecological value of de-extinction.
Predator risk 10% No invasive predators = 85. Active invasive predators present = 25. Invasive predators (e.g. rats, feral cats, mongoose) have driven multiple island reintroductions to failure. Their presence is a hard constraint on wild release.
Score labels

Numerical scores are converted to text labels for the radar chart and summary table:

75–100 → Good
55–74 → Moderate
35–54 → Low
0–34 → Very Low
3 De-Extinction Verdict Decision Tree
How the verdict is reached Decision logic

The verdict is determined by a sequential decision tree that applies gates in order. Each gate is evaluated using the genomic and ecological scores computed above. The first gate that resolves determines the outcome.

1
Genomic gate
If DNA similarity < 60%, the total number of gene edits required exceeds current technological capability. No further evaluation is performed.
→ NOT POSSIBLE
2
Invasive predator gate
If invasive predators are present in the target habitat, wild release is blocked regardless of other scores, since predator-naïve de-extincted populations have historically collapsed within years. If ecological score ≥ 45, captive revival remains viable. Otherwise de-extinction should not be attempted.
→ CAPTIVITY ONLY (if eco ≥ 45)   → DO NOT ATTEMPT (if eco < 45)
3
Wild release gate
If ecological composite ≥ 65, the habitat is sufficiently intact for wild release. The climate and biome scores then determine whether the candidate needs genetic modification before release, or can be relocated directly.
→ WILD — RELOCATE (climate ≥ 60 AND biome ≥ 60)
→ WILD — GMO NEEDED (climate or biome < 60)
4
Captive revival gate
If ecological composite ≥ 40 but below the wild release threshold, conditions support controlled captive revival pending habitat restoration or invasive species management.
→ CAPTIVITY ONLY
5
No-attempt gate
If ecological composite < 40, ecological conditions present unacceptable risks even for captive revival. De-extinction is theoretically possible but should not be pursued with the selected candidate.
→ DO NOT ATTEMPT
The five possible outcomes
NOT POSSIBLE
Genomic divergence too high. Editing burden exceeds current technology.
WILD — RELOCATE
Direct relocation to historic range. No significant genetic modification required.
WILD — GMO NEEDED
Wild release viable but targeted gene edits required (e.g. cold-adaptation loci).
CAPTIVITY ONLY
Revival feasible in controlled environment. Wild release requires habitat improvement.
DO NOT ATTEMPT
Technically possible but ecological risks make any reintroduction inadvisable.
