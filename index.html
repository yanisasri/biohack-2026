How to read the Species Distribution chart
What each dot means
Each dot is a recorded occurrence — a location where the species was known to live or where ecologically comparable habitat exists today. Dot size reflects habitat density: larger dots indicate higher-quality or more strongly suitable conditions at that site.
The three tabs
Historic — pre-extinction occurrence records from fossil pollen, museum specimens, and GBIF data. Shows the full range the species once occupied.
Modern — sites where ecologically comparable habitat survives today. This is where a de-extincted species could theoretically be released.
Overlap — both datasets together. Amber dots are today's viable zones; faded green are lost range. A small overlap signals greater adaptation challenges.
Why it matters
A large overlap between historic and modern distributions suggests the candidate species would encounter familiar ecological conditions. Minimal overlap means significant habitat mismatch — a key factor in the de-extinction viability score.
